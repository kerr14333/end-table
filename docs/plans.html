Joinery Details

Half-lap (leg crossing)

  • The two legs of each X cross at 60° (acute angle) — twice the 30° lean.
  • Crossing center is ≈ 13¾" up from the floor — about 15¾" from the foot measured along the board.
  • Lay the legs crossed, scribe the overlap, set a dado/router to exactly 3/8" (half of 3/4"), and remove the overlap from opposite faces so they interlock flush.
  • Glue the lap; clamp flat and check the frame sits without rocking.

Top edges (router)

  • Corners: 1⅛" radius — bandsaw/jigsaw close, then template or sand to the line.
  • Underside chamfer: 45° bearing-guided chamfer bit, depth set to 9/16", run around the whole perimeter (follows the rounded corners automatically). Leaves a 3/16" edge so the overhang reads thin and floats.
  • Top edge: a tiny 1/32" micro-bevel (chamfer bit barely engaged, or a few light block-plane passes) for a crisp upper arris.
  • Cherry tears out easily — take light passes and climb-cut the corners.

Stretcher joint (dowels)

  • The stretcher butts end-grain against each frame's inner face, so it is glued and dowelled — glue alone on end grain is weak.
  • Two 3/8" dowels per end (4 total), stacked ≈ 5/8" apart in the 1½" height and centered in the 3/4" thickness.
  • Drill ≈ 1/2" into the leg face and ≈ 3/4" into the stretcher end (dowel ≈ 1¼" long). Don't drill through the 3/4" leg.
  • Use a dowel jig (or drill the stretcher ends, add dowel centers, and transfer the marks to the legs). Dry-fit, then glue.

Top fasteners (figure-8)

  • Attach the top with figure-8 (desktop) fasteners — two per cleat (4 total), one near each end of each cleat.
  • Drill a shallow Forstner recess in the top face of each cleat — bit diameter ≈ the fastener’s width, depth = its thickness — so the fastener sits flush and the top still seats flat on the cleat.
  • Screw one lobe down into the cleat; the other lobe screws up into the underside of the top. Pilot every hole and keep the top screw short — don’t break through the 3/4" top.
  • Orient each fastener so it can pivot toward the top’s long edges (its long axis along the table’s length), letting the top expand and contract across its width.

Making the Legs on the Table Saw

All four legs are cut entirely on the table saw (plus a tapering jig that rides the rip fence). Each leg is a tapered parallelogram: both ends are cut at the same 30° angle so the foot and top seat flat, and the board narrows from top to foot. The four legs are two mirrored pairs — two that lean left (“\”) and two that lean right (“/”). Mark a face side and a reference edge on every blank, keep them oriented the same way against the fence and miter gauge, and cut every angle and depth in scrap first — cherry tears out badly.

Working with the Bosch 4100 jobsite saw. Two of its limits shape the plan below: (1) the table is small, so a 28" board crosscut on the miter gauge is poorly supported — for the 30° end cuts, use your miter saw instead (easier and safer; see step 2). (2) The 4100 is not designed to run a dado stack, so the half-lap is cut with a router (preferred) or by nibbling with a single blade — not a dado set (see step 4). The rip and the tapering-jig cuts are well within the saw’s comfort zone; just add infeed/outfeed support for the long blanks.

Why 30° for everything. The legs lean 30° from vertical, so the end cuts are 30° off square and the angled half-lap is (90 − 2×30) = 30° off square. Both land on the same positive stop of the POWERTEC 71391 miter gauge (stops every 5° to 60°, plus 22.5°), so you set the gauge once and never chase a freehand angle.

1 · Rip the blanks

  • From 3/4" cherry, rip four blanks 3" wide × 28" long. The extra width (3" vs. the 2½" finished top) and length (28" vs. ≈ 26" finished) give room for the angled ends and the taper.
  • Blade square to the table (90°). Joint or rip one long edge straight first — that becomes the reference edge the jig and miter gauge register against.

2 · Cut the 30° ends

Both ends of a leg are parallel and cut 30° off square, with the blade/saw vertical (90°) — no bevel tilt, because the leg leans in the plane of its wide face. The miter saw is the easier tool here on the small Bosch table.

  • Miter saw (recommended): swing it to 30°, lay the blank flat with its reference edge against the fence, and cut one end. For the second end, flip the board face-for-face (same edge to the fence, roll it over) and cut at the same 30° so the two ends stay parallel — measure ≈ 26" point-to-point along the long edge first.
  • Table saw (alternative): miter gauge on the 30° positive stop, blade vertical, with a tall auxiliary fence for support. Cut one end, flip face-for-face, cut the other. Note the 28" blank overhangs the small 4100 table — support the off-end and go slowly.
  • Cut two legs at this setting. For the other two (the mirrored partners), set the saw to the opposite 30° so the ends slope the other way.
  • Don’t worry about the sharp finished corners. Once tapered, the long point at each end comes out an acute ≈58–62° (just 60° ± the taper) — that’s a result of the 30° cut, not something you set or measure. The only number you dial in is 30° off square.

3 · Taper 2½" → 1"

  • Use a shop-made (or adjustable) tapering jig that rides the rip fence; the leg sits in the jig and the jig carries it past the blade.
  • The taper removes 1½" of width over the ≈ 26" length (≈ 3.3°). Taper one edge only so the other stays a straight reference edge: set the jig so the foot end is offset 1½" relative to the top end, leaving 2½" at the top and 1" at the foot.
  • Keep the same reference edge and face against the jig on all four legs so the tapers match. (Want a symmetric taper instead? Remove 3/4" from each edge — but then re-true a reference edge first.)
  • Take light passes; finish the sawn edge with a couple of hand-plane strokes to remove saw marks.

4 · Half-lap at the crossing — scribe, don’t calculate

Because the legs cross at an angle the notch is skewed, so trace it from the actual parts rather than measuring:

  1. Cut the ends and tapers on all four legs first.
  2. Lay one mirrored pair in its final crossed position — feet ≈ 15⅞" apart, crossing ≈ 13¾" up (≈ 15⅞" from each foot along the board) — and trace each leg onto the other where they overlap. The two notches go on opposite faces so the legs interlock flush.
  3. Set the cutter height to exactly 3/8". Foolproof check: notch two scraps and nest them — together they must equal one full 3/4" thickness with the faces flush; adjust height until they do.
  4. Remove the waste between your scribed lines. The 4100 won’t take a dado stack, so:
    • Router (recommended): a 3/8"-or-larger straight bit set to a 3/8" depth stop, guided by a straightedge clamped along each scribed line; square the corners with a chisel. Cleanest result on a jobsite saw with no dado.
    • Table saw (alternative): miter gauge on the same 30° stop (it aligns with your scribed lines), a single blade raised to 3/8", and nibble out the waste in repeated passes. Pare the floor flat with a chisel or router plane.
  5. Repeat for the second pair (mirror the angle).

Dry-fit each lap before glue: the two legs must sit flush and coplanar with no rock, and the four feet/tips should land where the plan shows. Then glue the lap and clamp flat.

Assembly Sequence

  1. Top: glue up the panel, flatten, trim to 20 × 8¼. Round the 4 corners, rout the underside 45° chamfer, then the 1/32" top micro-bevel. Sand to 220.
  2. Leg blanks: rip four ~3"-wide blanks, mark the taper (2½" at the top to 1" at the foot), and cut/joint the tapers.
  3. Half-laps: lay out and cut the lap on each leg; dry-assemble the two X-frames and confirm the tips and feet are coplanar (no rock), then glue.
  4. Stretcher: join the two X-frames with the 3/4" stretcher at the crossing — glue + two 3/8" dowels per end into each frame's inner face (drill ~1/2" into the leg, ~3/4" into the stretcher end; see Joinery Details). This ties the base together at the crossing.
  5. Cleats: bevel the two long bottom edges (1/4" at 45°, tablesaw or chamfer bit), leaving the ends square where the leg tips bear. Then fasten a cleat under each end of the top — glue and screw it rigidly down onto the two leg tips at that end. This ties the frames together at the top.
  6. Attach the top with figure-8 fasteners — two recessed flush into each cleat’s top face, one screw down to the cleat and one up into the top (see Joinery Details and the note below).
  7. Finish: natural Danish oil or wiped satin poly. Cherry will darken to a warm reddish-brown that complements the chair.

Attaching the top — allow for wood movement. The solid 8¼" top will expand/contract across its width (~1/16" seasonally). Each end of the top carries a cleat (~7 × 3⅝ × 3/4) that the leg tips bear on: screw the cleat rigidly to the leg tips, then attach the top to the cleats with figure-8 (desktop) fasteners — two per cleat, recessed flush into the cleat top, one screw down to the cleat and one up into the top. The metal “8” pivots slightly so the top can float across its width. The cleats also tie the two frames together at the top. Never screw the legs straight up into the top — that can crack the panel.

Design Notes

  • No breadboard ends. The top is a single solid panel; at 8¼" wide it is not very cup-prone, and breadboard ends would add an advanced joint and change the clean look. Can be added if desired.
  • Stability: a ≈15⅞" lengthwise foot stance plus a widened ~6¼" across-width stance. The base is tied together at the top (cleats) and bottom (stretcher), so it doesn’t rack or wobble. Tipping check: on the weak axis it would take ≈ 35 lb set right at the edge to tip it — a drink, lamp, or plant is a wide safety margin.
  • Tools used: tablesaw, router, miter saw, and hand tools only.
